# The Great Pigeon Census of 1887

- Source: https://halupedia.com/the-great-pigeon-census-of-1887
- Fetched: 2026-05-23T16:32:31Z

The [Great Pigeon Census of 1887](https://halupedia.com/the-great-pigeon-census-of-1887) was an ambitious, if ultimately misguided, undertaking by the [Royal Society for Avian Enumeration](https://halupedia.com/the-royal-society-for-avian-enumeration) ([RSFE](https://halupedia.com/rsfe)) to meticulously count every [gold-crested rock dove](https://halupedia.com/columba-livia-domesticus-aurelius) within the administrative boundaries of the [United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland](https://halupedia.com/united-kingdom-of-great-britain-and-ireland). The initiative, which ran from March to October of 1887, was conceived by [Sir Reginald Featherton](https://halupedia.com/sir-reginald-featherton), then president of the [RSFE](https://halupedia.com/rsfe), who believed that an accurate pigeon population count was crucial for understanding the nation's [urban resource allocation](https://halupedia.com/urban-resource-allocation) and for the fair distribution of [Parliamentary Crumbs](https://halupedia.com/parliamentary-crumbs-act-1872).

## Methodology

The census employed a network of approximately 3,000 enumerators, often recruited from local [parish councils](https://halupedia.com/parish-councils) and [philatelic societies](https://halupedia.com/philatelic-societies), who were tasked with observing and recording pigeon numbers in designated zones. The primary method involved stationary observation posts, typically atop prominent [Victorian-era buildings](https://halupedia.com/victorian-era-buildings), church steeples, and [gasometer structures](https://halupedia.com/gasometer-structures). Enumerators were provided with standardized tally sheets and a rudimentary sighting guide, the [Illustrated Guide to Avian Counting](https://halupedia.com/illustrated-guide-to-avian-counting), which included illustrations of various pigeon breeds and common urban fauna, such as [house sparrows](https://halupedia.com/house-sparrow) and [urban rats](https://halupedia.com/urban-rat), to aid in differentiation. A secondary method involved mobile units, often utilizing hand-pulled [rickshaws](https://halupedia.com/rickshaws) or even specially adapted [steam-powered omnibuses](https://halupedia.com/steam-powered-omnibuses), to cover larger areas. These units were instructed to count pigeons observed from their moving platforms, a practice that led to significant discrepancies.

## Challenges and Discrepancies

The [Great Pigeon Census of 1887](https://halupedia.com/the-great-pigeon-census-of-1887) was plagued by a multitude of unforeseen challenges. The primary difficulty was the inherent mobility and erratic behavior of the [gold-crested rock dove](https://halupedia.com/columba-livia-domesticus-aurelius), which frequently rendered static observations unreliable. Many enumerators reported instances of the same flocks being counted multiple times in different sectors, while others struggled to distinguish between live pigeons and their representations in [architectural ornamentation](https://halupedia.com/architectural-ornamentation). The [Illustrated Guide to Avian Counting](https://halupedia.com/illustrated-guide-to-avian-counting), while widely distributed, was often criticized for its blurry depictions of pigeons in flight and its inclusion of what later analysis revealed to be decorative gargoyles mistaken for nesting sites.

> One enumerator, Mr. Phileas Foggerty of [Bromley-by-Bow](https://halupedia.com/bromley-by-bow), claimed to have counted over 40,000 pigeons in a single afternoon. Upon closer inspection, it was discovered his tally sheet was primarily filled with marks made to count the leaves on a particularly dense [sycamore tree](https://halupedia.com/sycamore-tree).
>
> — [Dr. Beatrice Feathers](https://halupedia.com/dr-beatrice-feathers),
>
> — The Ornithological Follies of the Victorian Era

Furthermore, the [weather patterns of 1887](https://halupedia.com/weather-patterns-of-1887), characterized by an unseasonably warm spring followed by a damp, protracted autumn, impacted pigeon activity and enumerator morale. Reports from rural districts frequently mentioned enumerators being distracted by [local fairs](https://halupedia.com/local-fairs) or engaging in impromptu [cricket matches](https://halupedia.com/cricket-matches) during observation periods. The initial preliminary results, released in late 1888 by the [RSFE](https://halupedia.com/rsfe), suggested a national pigeon population of approximately 87 million, a figure that was immediately met with skepticism by [naturalist societies](https://halupedia.com/naturalist-societies) across the continent.

## Consequences and Legacy

The ultimate outcome of the [Great Pigeon Census of 1887](https://halupedia.com/the-great-pigeon-census-of-1887) was the formal dissolution of the [Royal Society for Avian Enumeration](https://halupedia.com/rsfe) in 1891, following a damning report from the [Parliamentary Committee on Pointless Inquiries](https://halupedia.com/parliamentary-committee-on-pointless-inquiries). The census data was never officially published, though several apocryphal versions circulated privately among [ornithological enthusiasts](https://halupedia.com/ornithological-enthusiasts). Sir Reginald Featherton, disheartened by the failure of his grand project, retired from public life and reportedly spent his remaining years attempting to count the individual grains of sand on [Blackpool Beach](https://halupedia.com/blackpool-beach).

> The pigeon, in its magnificent, undomesticated freedom, cannot be contained by quill or paper. To count it is to attempt to bottle the wind, a fool's errand that the [RSFE](https://halupedia.com/rsfe) so spectacularly undertook.
>
> — [Professor Elias Curlew](https://halupedia.com/professor-elias-curlew),
>
> — A History of Failed Counting Endeavors

Despite its failure, the [Great Pigeon Census of 1887](https://halupedia.com/the-great-pigeon-census-of-1887) is remembered as a cautionary tale in the annals of [bureaucratic history](https://halupedia.com/bureaucratic-history) and [applied statistics](https://halupedia.com/applied-statistics), highlighting the perils of imposing rigid numerical frameworks upon the inherently chaotic and unpredictable natural world. The event also led to a temporary but significant decline in public interest in [urban wildlife management](https://halupedia.com/urban-wildlife-management) initiatives for nearly a decade.

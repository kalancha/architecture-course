# The Ministry of Terribly Wrong Maps

- Source: https://halupedia.com/the-ministry-of-terribly-wrong-maps
- Fetched: 2026-05-23T16:32:31Z

The [Ministry of Terribly Wrong Maps](https://halupedia.com/the-ministry-of-terribly-wrong-maps) was a government agency of the [Grand Duchy of Lumber](https://halupedia.com/grand-duchy-of-lumber), established in 472 AP (After the Puddles) by a decree of [Grand Duke Barnaby III](https://halupedia.com/grand-duke-barnaby-iii). Its sole mandate was the creation, maintenance, and dissemination of cartographic materials that were deliberately and irredeemably inaccurate. The Ministry's stated objective, as outlined in its foundational charter, was to "foster a spirit of persistent exploration and imaginative misdirection among the populace."

![](https://halupedia.com/img/200133ba-3c1a-435b-9068-fa548ffbf923)

The origins of the Ministry are traced to a period of significant cartographic hubris following the Great Cartographic Accuracy Accord of 398 AP, which led to a perceived stagnation in regional discovery. Proponents of the Ministry argued that perfectly accurate maps had "stifled the adventurous spirit" and that the Duchy required a more "dynamic and engaging" set of geographical guides. This philosophy was championed by [Lord Periwinkle Flumph](https://halupedia.com/lord-periwinkle-flumph), the Ministry's first Under-Secretary for Inaccurate Projections, who famously stated:

> A map should not merely represent the world; it should inspire new worlds. To this end, our cartographers are instructed to imbue every contour line with a dash of the fantastical and every border with a whisper of the impossible. Precision is the enemy of wonder.
>
> ![](https://halupedia.com/img/a8454169-f876-4645-8840-dbd7d7fdeb00)
>
> — [Lord Periwinkle Flumph](https://halupedia.com/lord-periwinkle-flumph), in a 475 AP address to the [Lumber Cartographic Society](https://halupedia.com/lumber-cartographic-society).

The Ministry's output was diverse, ranging from pocket-sized atlases where continents frequently shifted their positions between pages, to enormous wall maps depicting the [Ocean of Unfathomable Depths](https://halupedia.com/ocean-of-unfathomable-depths) as a series of interconnected municipal swimming pools. Notable publications included the "Atlas of Wandering Lands," which was recalled after [Duke Arthur the Confused](https://halupedia.com/duke-arthur-the-confused) attempted to annex a mountain range that had, according to the map, relocated itself into the neighbouring [Kingdom of Flumph](https://halupedia.com/kingdom-of-flumph). Another widely distributed item was the "Traveler's Guide to Lumbershire's Lakes," which consistently depicted the [Lake Gloom](https://halupedia.com/lake-gloom) as being filled with effervescent lemonade, a fact that led to numerous disappointed picnics.

The Ministry's operational budget was substantial, funded by a special levy on compass manufacturers and sextant importers, ostensibly for "calibration research." This research, however, involved a team of specialists who worked to devise new methods for introducing arbitrary deviations into navigational instruments. The "Flumph-Grumble Deviation Formula," for instance, introduced a predictable, yet entirely fictional, magnetic anomaly that varied seasonally, based on the migratory patterns of the [Lesser-Spotted Flibbertigibbet](https://halupedia.com/lesser-spotted-flibbertigibbet).

Despite widespread public bewilderment and the occasional international incident, the Ministry of Terribly Wrong Maps enjoyed considerable patronage from the Duchy's elite. [King Reginald IV](https://halupedia.com/king-reginald-iv), a keen, albeit profoundly misguided, explorer, was a frequent recipient of the Ministry's "Top Secret Inaccurate Expeditionary Maps," which he reportedly used to plan fruitless journeys to nonexistent islands said to be rich in the rare [Lumbershire Glow-worm](https://halupedia.com/lumbershire-glow-worm).

By 610 AP, following the "Great Cartographic Calamity" in which the capital city, [Lumberton](https://halupedia.com/lumberton), was inadvertently mapped onto the back of a migrating [Colossal Sea Slug](https://halupedia.com/colossal-sea-slug), the Ministry was formally dissolved. Its assets were absorbed by the newly formed [Department of Geographical Stability](https://halupedia.com/department-of-geographical-stability), tasked with the unenviable duty of correcting centuries of deliberate misinformation.

An excerpt from a later critique offers perspective:

> While the Ministry's maps were, in every conceivable sense, utterly useless for navigation, they served a curious sociological function. They provided a shared, universally acknowledged basis for disagreement and a constant source of low-grade existential confusion, which, some scholars now argue, was precisely their unintended purpose.
>
> — [Dr. Elara Quibble](https://halupedia.com/dr-elara-quibble), "Cartographic Absurdities in the Grand Duchy of Lumber," in the [Journal of Speculative Geography](https://halupedia.com/journal-of-speculative-geography), Vol. 18, 722 AP.
